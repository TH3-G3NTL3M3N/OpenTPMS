Overall Architecture Approaches

Three ways to structure the hardware, firmware, and manufacturing. All share the same requirements — the difference is how we get there.

A

Module-First (Recommended)

Build entirely around a pre-certified nRF52 module (e.g., Raytac MDBT42Q). The module IS the brain — our custom PCB is just a carrier board connecting the module to the pressure sensor, accelerometer, supercap, and battery holder.

Pros

  • Fastest path to working prototype — module handles all RF, antenna, crystal, decoupling
  • Pre-certified (FCC/CE/IC) — skip $15-20k in testing
  • Simple 2-layer carrier PCB — cheap to fab, easy to design for a first-timer
  • Module datasheet tells you exactly how to connect everything
  • Can move to bare-chip in v2 if volume justifies it

Cons

  • Module is ~$3-5 vs ~$2 for bare nRF52 chip — adds ~$2/unit
  • Module footprint is slightly larger than bare chip (10x15mm vs 5x5mm)
  • Less control over antenna placement — use what the module gives you

Best for: Learning PCB design while actually shipping a product. Minimizes risk on the hardest part (RF).

B

Bare-Chip Custom

Design a fully custom PCB with a bare nRF52832 chip, external crystal, matching network, and PCB trace antenna. Everything from scratch.

Pros

  • Smallest possible footprint — bare chip is 5x5mm QFN
  • Full control over antenna design and placement
  • Lowest BOM cost at volume (~$1-2 saved per unit)
  • Maximum design flexibility

Cons

  • Requires RF design experience — impedance matching, antenna tuning
  • 4-layer PCB minimum for proper RF routing — 2-3x fab cost
  • Must do full FCC/CE certification ($15-20k+)
  • Higher risk of RF issues in first revision
  • Steep learning curve for someone new to PCB design

Best for: Experienced hardware engineers or high-volume production (10k+ units).

C

Dev Board Prototype → Custom Production

Start with an nRF52 dev board (like Adafruit Feather nRF52) wired to sensors on a breadboard. Validate firmware and algorithms. Then design the custom PCB for production.

Pros

  • Validate firmware before committing to hardware — cheapest way to test algorithms
  • Can test pressure sensor, accelerometer, BLE/ANT+ on your desk immediately
  • Zero PCB design needed for phase 1

Cons

  • Dev board doesn't fit inside a tire — can't do real-world testing until custom PCB
  • Two full design phases instead of one — doubles timeline
  • Risk of firmware assumptions that don't translate to final hardware
  • Dev boards use different GPIO pins, power rails — firmware needs porting anyway

Best for: Pure software people who want to validate algorithms before touching hardware.
